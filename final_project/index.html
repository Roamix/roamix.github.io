Correlations between weather and vehicle collisions

As the interactive section suggests, a large quantity of the data is unspecified or consists of independent human errors. By crossreferencing the collision data with weather dataset, we explore any hidden correllations that may exists between the two datasets.

Dates organized by how much precipitation (rain, drizzle etc.) has occured compared to average number of collisions on those dates shows that, the more precipitation there is, the looser the correlation gets.

Instead, a suspicion refered to in the introductory video is explored: Namely, dates binned by the daily peak temperature vs the average number of collisions on those dates.

Warm days - which tend also to be sunny days - have a clear tendency to have a higher frequency og collisions. A reasonable explanation can be that focus and pay more attention in bad weather and inversely relax in nicer weather. Furthermore bright sun and glare can play a large contributing factor.

Any reasonable driver primed for certain weather conditions may be more likely to be involved in a collision if the weather takes a large shift throughout the day. Days with greatest temperature shifts have - on average - a higher collision frequency.

Heatmaps
